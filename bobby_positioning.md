# Bobby Kenner — Positioning Notes

## How AI has changed my design process / how I want to be positioned

My first instinct now is: what data are we dealing with and how does it relate to the problem we're solving and how can it add value. I spend a lot of time polishing interactions — things that took a while in Figma or other prototyping tools. It's so much easier to preview an interaction and iterate on it in real time and feel it/refine it. You can also polish it in ways that engineers never really did before (or was too annoying to ask for or often deprioritized) — like transitions and animations. Not so much "delightful" ones, but highly functional ones that take many many iterations to get right.

Designers are well positioned to do this because taste is the last skill in EPD. I feel like I have that taste — not so much in the traditional sense (putting lipstick on a pig) but more in the human/computer interaction sense. When someone uses a product, I want them to not necessarily think "a designer did this" because I believe good design is mostly invisible, but going that extra mile to show you care is important. I put emotion into that side of things, which I think is a niche in design — it's somewhere between product, product design, and engineering.

I'm not a brand guy, or at least I don't think I am — until maybe I feel passionate enough about a mission or I can see where a brand is trying to mislead. I don't mind being the guy pulling the brand language into a cohesive design system that uses the visual elements in a way that the brand team likes — while also trying to bring a layer of that brand into the design system itself through expressive components.

## 2-bullet summary

- I rarely open Figma to start anymore. I prototype in code, which means I can feel and iterate on interactions in real time — transitions, timing, functional motion — the kind of polish that used to get deprioritized or was too tedious to ask engineering for. I also document as I go: governance notes, rigid vs. flexible patterns, design rationale baked into the prototype so engineers have context, not just specs. Handoff is built in from the first pass, not bolted on at the end.
- At Superhuman I started seeing design not as a handoff but as a capability that should live inside the engineering workflow. I helped shape their prototyping platform and was building a centralized design skill that could be invoked across the full product delivery process — evaluative in Figma to start, but scoped to expand into validative research, design system coherence, accessibility, and content. The thing I kept coming back to: the tone of that skill matters as much as what it knows.

## Additional context / not yet in bullets

**AI philosophy:**
Bobby is a techno-human optimist — genuinely believes in AI but sees its flaws clearly, and gets frustrated specifically when AI responses get "cheaper" (vague, asks unnecessary questions, stops being proactive). That frustration is rooted in a belief that everyone — especially the underprivileged — deserves access to the same quality of AI assistance. Has used ChatGPT (via Grammarly), Claude, and others extensively; understands the strengths and tradeoffs of major LLMs from real use, not theory. Primarily uses Claude and Claude Code.

**What he'd want to know about a new company:**
- How have they actually embraced AI — not just in product, but internally?
- Does everyone have a clear, shared understanding of what AI is for and what it isn't for?
- Are they using it alongside humans, or as a replacement? That distinction matters a lot.
- This is a filter for new roles, not just a curiosity.
